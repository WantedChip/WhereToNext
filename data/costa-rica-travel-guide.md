## Summary
Costa Rica offers an unusually varied mix of rainforests, volcanoes, beaches, wildlife, waterfalls, and adventure activities within a relatively compact country. Based on a 12-day road trip through San José, Manuel Antonio, La Fortuna, Monteverde, and Guanacaste, the article highlights outdoor experiences, notable stops, accommodation ideas, dining recommendations, and practical travel observations.

## Things To Do
- Rappel through the rainforest and descend five waterfalls near La Fortuna.
- Go white-water rafting on class III rapids while watching for monkeys, iguanas, and birds.
- Detour to Catarata del Toro to see a 270-foot waterfall and explore the surrounding falls.
- Walk the trails and beaches of Manuel Antonio National Park, where jungle and coastline meet.
- Cross six suspended bridges above the rainforest at Mistico Arenal Hanging Bridges Park.
- Join a guided night walk in Monteverde to search for snakes, tarantulas, frogs, birds, and insects.
- Explore Guanacaste beaches such as Tamarindo, Conchal, and Flamingo, each with a different atmosphere.
- Scuba dive off Guanacaste to look for rays, fish, reef sharks, and other marine life.
- Wander the pedestrian-only streets of Las Catalinas and enjoy its beachside setting and spa experiences.
- Zipline through Monteverde's cloud forest on seven cables, including lines reaching up to 2,460 feet.
- Relax in La Fortuna's hot springs, with options ranging from natural river spots to full-service resorts.
- Visit the Sloth Watching Trail near La Fortuna for a convenient chance to see wild sloths.
- Ride horseback through the jungle to La Fortuna Waterfall and swim in the pool below the falls.
- Stop in Jacó for its lively surf-town atmosphere and visit nearby Playa Hermosa for larger waves and a quieter setting.
- Look for the large crocodiles gathered beneath the well-known roadside bridge on the route toward Manuel Antonio.
- Stay near Arenal Volcano and enjoy views of the mountain from a room or hot spring.

## Notes From The Article
- The trip lasted 12 days and covered five main areas: San José, Manuel Antonio, La Fortuna, Monteverde, and Guanacaste Province.
- San José is the country's capital and a common arrival point; notable city sights include Central Avenue, the National Museum, the Pre-Columbian Gold Museum, the National Theater, and Mercado Central.
- Manuel Antonio lies about three hours southwest of San José and works well for travelers who want beaches, jungle scenery, and wildlife in one area.
- La Fortuna was the author's top destination and combined Arenal Volcano views with rafting, rappelling, hanging bridges, hot springs, wildlife watching, and horseback riding.
- Monteverde is known for its protected cloud forest and rich plant and animal life, but the roads there had more potholes and the route from La Fortuna was winding.
- Guanacaste was highlighted for its long coastline, surfing, resorts, wildlife, diving, and variety of beach towns.
- The roads leading to Catarata del Toro were paved but extremely steep, and the return walk from the waterfall involved many steps.
- Separate waterfalls around the Catarata del Toro area may charge individual entrance fees.
- Manuel Antonio National Park offers trails and beaches, including a narrow jungle-covered peninsula between two stretches of sand.
- Rain did not prevent the Monteverde night walk or ziplining experience, though weather conditions affected the visit.
- The Sloth Watching Trail cost about $10 per person, while a guided visit was about $50; the authors saw two sloths within roughly 30 minutes.
- Horseback riding to La Fortuna Waterfall included steep sections, and the horses occasionally slipped on the road, so it may not suit nervous riders.
- The crocodile bridge is best treated as a convenient roadside stop rather than a destination worth a special detour.
- Renting a car allowed the authors to travel from San José to Liberia, and they found most roads easy to navigate and in good condition.
- A travel agency helped organize accommodation, transport, activities, and assistance when the rental-car key broke far from the rental office.
- Hotel San Bada was valued for being steps from Manuel Antonio National Park and having direct access toward the beach.
- Hotel Fondavela provided a lodge-style stay near the Monteverde Cloud Forest Biological Preserve.
- Santarena Hotel in car-free Las Catalinas offered ocean views and a rooftop pool.
- Arenal Manoa Resort was a standout stay because of its views of Arenal Volcano and access to hot springs.
- Ronny's Place near Manuel Antonio was recommended for dinner with Pacific sunset views, though it sits a short distance off the main road on an easy gravel track.
- La Saca Restaurant offered Costa Rican food and views of Arenal Volcano.
- The Tree House Restaurant in Monteverde looked distinctive because it appeared to be built around a large tree, although the authors did not eat there.
- Ponciana in Las Catalinas was convenient for guests at Santarena Hotel and served traditional Costa Rican seafood dishes.
